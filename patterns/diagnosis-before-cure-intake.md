# Diagnosis-Before-Cure Intake

**Source:** Corey Ganim AI Tools Assessment thread  
**URL:** https://x.com/coreyganim/status/2077788148285792662  
**Author:** Corey Ganim / @coreyganim  
**Reviewed:** 2026-07-17  

## Pattern

Start with a structured diagnostic conversation about actual daily work before recommending tools, automation, process changes, or support. The goal is to understand lived friction, not to pitch a preselected solution.

## Intake Questions

Use concrete prompts:

- Walk me through yesterday.
- What tasks do you dread?
- Where does work pile up?
- What repeats every week?
- What waits on one person?
- What gets copied between systems?
- What do people ask you for again and again?
- What do you avoid because it takes too much effort?
- What would save the most time if it were easier?

Capture the conversation as a transcript or detailed notes. The transcript is evidence; the recommendation is a derived artifact.

## Analysis Pass

Turn the transcript into:

1. Pain points, in the user's words.
2. Repeated workflows.
3. Bottlenecks and handoffs.
4. Existing tools/systems involved.
5. Privacy, security, accessibility, budget, and maintenance constraints.
6. Candidate fixes: process change, documentation, off-the-shelf tool, automation, or human support.
7. Effort/impact ranking.
8. Quick-win plan.
9. Open questions that must be answered before implementation.

## Report Shape

Keep the output short:

- executive summary
- friction map
- effort vs impact matrix
- recommended options
- quick-win plan
- risks and constraints
- decision prompt: what is most urgent, DIY or get help, and timeline

## Why It Works

People often ask for tools before they have named the work. A diagnosis-first intake catches cases where the right answer is not automation at all: delete steps, clarify ownership, write a checklist, centralize knowledge, or change a handoff.

The pattern also keeps recommendations grounded. A tool suggestion must point back to an observed friction point and a constraint, not just to a generic category.

## Caveats

Do not treat a transcript-to-recommendation pass as truth. Validate recommendations with the people doing the work, especially when the domain is sensitive or the change affects privacy, safety, money, health, or legal obligations.

Tool directories are discovery aids, not due diligence. Check data handling, accessibility, integrations, pricing, support, exportability, and failure modes before adopting any tool.
